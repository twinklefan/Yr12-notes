Mechanics

    Scalars & Vectors

  • Vectors are quantities with size and direction
  • Scalars only have size

    • Speed/Velocity

  • Speed discribes how quickly an object's position changes
  • Unit: meters per second or m/s or ms⁻¹
  • v = d/t

    • Acceleration

  • Acceleration discribes how quickly an object's speed changes
  • Unit: meters per second, per second or m/s/s or ms⁻²
  • When an object accelerates against its velocity, we say it decelerates
  • a = v/t

    • Graphs

  • Distance-time graph
  • The slope of a ditance-time graph is the velocity
  • The steeper the slope, the higher the velocity
  • Speed-time graph
  • The slope of a speed-time graph is the acceleration
  • The area is the distance
  • The steeper the slope, the higher the acceleration

    • Kinematic equations

  • Kinematic equations are used to discribe objects with constant acceleration (including 0!)
  • They contain a mix of these values:
  • v = vi + at (missing d)
  • d = vit + at² (missing vf)
  • vf² = vi² + 2ad (missing t)
  • d = 0.5(vf + vi)t (missing a)

    • Scaling vectors

  • Vectors have 2 properties...
  • Magnitude indicated by length
  • Direction indicated by orientation
  • To scale a vector (affect its size), we multiply it by a scalar
  • Multipling a vector by a negative number causes it to point in the opposite direction

    • Equating vectors

  • Similar to scalars, vectors form equations. To do this we can use these techniques
  • Loops/circles are 0, Vectors forming a closed path are equal to 0
  • Equivalent paths: Vectors or sets of vectors that start and finish in the same place are equal

    • Relative velocity

  • Velocity is always relative to something. No velocity is truer than another
  • The relative velocity between two objects depends on whether they're in the same direction or opposing
  • Same direction velocities subtract
  • Opposing velocities add

    • Gravity

  • Earth's gravity accelerates all objects downward at 9.8ms⁻²
  • This means an object's velocity would increase by 9.8ms⁻¹ per second
  • This is independent of the object's mass
  • If an object experiences air resistance however, it's total acceleration will depend on it's mass

    • Projectile motion

  • An object in projectile motion has both horizontal and vertical motion
  • For simplicity we assume, there is no air resistance, the only force acting in flight is gravity and gravity is uniform and downward

    • Force

  • A force is an influence that acts to accelerate objects, measured in Newtons (N)
  • Newtons 1st law: An object at rest will only change its motion (Velocity), if a force acts upon it
  • Newton's 2nd law: When a force acts upon an object, it will accelerate proportional to its mass
  • Newton's 3rd law: Forces exist in equal and opposite pairs

    • Hooke's law

  • We can describe the force producted when a material is deformed as...
  • Stiffness describes the Newtons of force a material exerts, for every metre of displacement
  • The slope of the graph is the stiffness (k)
  • Following elastic deformation the material returns to its original shape
  • Following plastic deformation the material is permanently defomed

    • Circular motion

  • Objects in circular motion are described using the following terms...
  • Radius (r): half circle width
  • Diameter (2r): circle width
  • Circumference (2πr): length of one lap/perimeter of circle
  • Revolution: complete one lap
  • Period (T): seconds per revolution
  • Frequency (f): revolutions per second

    • Centripetal acceleration

  • As an object changes direction its velocity changes (even if the size of the velocity does not!)
  • This is called the centripetal acceleration
  • A force or combination of forces providing centripetal acceleration are called centripetal forces
  • Centripetal acceleration points to the center
  • Velocity points tangential

    • Torque

  • A force is an influence that acts to accelerate an object
  • A torque is an influence that acts to angularly accelerate an object
  • It has units of Newton metres (Nm)

    • Equilibrium

  • Objects in mechanical equilibrium experience no change in their motion (linear or rotational)
  • For this to occur the net force and net torque must equal zero
  • Note that an object in equilibrium doesn't need to be at rest
  • For systems with multiple pivots, any pivot can be chosen for analysis
  • Because torques at r=0 are zero, they will be zero out of equations

    • Centre of mass

  • Centre of mass describes the average position of a set of objects, weighted by their masses
  • Two objects on a pivot will balance at their centre of mass

    • Energy

  • Energy describes the potential for an object to change its motion
  • The unit is Joules (J)
  • Energu comes in many forms, we will consider the following...

    • Conservation of energy

  • Energy is never created or destroyed, only transferred into different forms
  • This idea is called conservation of energy

    • Work

  • When energy has been used, we say that work has been performed
  • Work is essentially another term for energy, it has the same unit, Joules (J)

    • Power

  • Power is the rate of energy consumption
  • It is measured in Joules per second, which are commonly called Watts (W)

    • Momentum

  • Momentum is a measurement of motion that takes into account both mass and velocity
  • It has a unit of kgms⁻¹
  • A bee and a car might travel at the same velocity, but have very different momentums due to their considerable mass difference

    • Impulse

  • The longer a collision takes, the smaller the force
  • We call this impulse

    • Conservation of momentum

  • The total momentum of a set of objects will only change if an external force acts upon them
  • This is called conservation of momentum

    • What is an external force?

  • Consider a falling apple...
  • As it falls, it's momentum increases. We could say that momentum isn't conserved because gravity is an external force
  • An external force is a force applied by an object whose momentum we've ignored. Often because it's impractical or impossible to measure
  • In the case of the apple, we've ignored the equal and opposite momentum the apple's gravity has imparted to the earth!
  • Momentum appears to not be conserved only when part of a system is ignored
